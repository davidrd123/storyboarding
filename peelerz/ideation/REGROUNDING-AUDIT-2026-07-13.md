# Peelerz re-grounding audit — almost restarting from the human material

**Date:** 2026-07-13  
**Status:** Working orientation, not a new campaign law  
**Primary grounding:** Ben's Discord messages, the treatment source, and the July 13 walk-and-talk

## Why this audit exists

The project developed a substantial creativity apparatus before the human conversation had been captured and understood. That apparatus produced useful research, language, seeds, and images, but it also began deciding what the problem was. This audit reverses that order.

The treatment, Ben's actual ask, and the [working merged transcript](voice-transcription/2026-07-13/WORKING-MERGED-TRANSCRIPT.md) are treated as Layer 0. Everything developed afterward—the campaign-law theories, constraint space, precedent dossier, fragment store, 88-seed archive, rankings, treatment, and visual probes—is treated as a candidate response that must re-earn relevance.

The purpose is not to condemn the previous work. It is to separate:

- what the human material actually supports;
- what remains a productive hypothesis;
- what is useful craft knowledge but not creative direction;
- what was premature closure;
- what the old process failed to notice.

## Reset conclusion

The old work mainly tried to solve this problem:

> What single Peelerz-specific law can organize the transformations and make the campaign ownable?

The human material points to a looser and more immediate assignment:

> Starting from these youthful encounters and the rough transformation beats, give Ben a rich, surprising, visually specific field of possibilities—some directly usable, some reference-driven, some excessive enough to unlock the room.

Those are not the same task.

Product logic, a unifying law, one-frame legibility, and campaign ownership may become useful during convergence. They should not define the imaginative field in advance. Ben explicitly says the job is about David's brain, not the technology; that only where the talent are met is fixed; and that the transformation space can be anything. The walk-and-talk repeatedly returns to going off script, inventing connective stories, trying historically and culturally specific styles, making things longer than the final spot, and including ideas that may chiefly delight Ben rather than survive the edit.

The restart should therefore be **human-led, character-aware, reference-rich, and permissive first**. Machinery can assist later, if it answers a felt need.

## A fresh read of the assignment

### What is actually fixed

Ben's Discord note is clearer than the machinery that followed it: “the only things that are set are where we meet the talent.” Those meeting points are the back of a car, high-school football bleachers, campus steps, and the Peelerz Islands finale.

The treatment supplies a current version of what happens in those places, but the walk-and-talk treats those actions as material to think with, not sacred functions. The car peel, giant hand, parachute, Ani-Mango-Raul, mouth dive, Doctor Strange transitions, underwater world, and floating islands are footholds and prompts. They are not all equally immutable.

The earlier engine quietly narrowed the task to four functional questions:

- how Tina's enclosure peels and launches her;
- how Raul's peeling constructs an avatar;
- how the couple's connection removes layers of the world;
- how those grammars combine in a peelable finale.

That is a coherent derived brief, but it is narrower than Ben's actual permission. It should be one possible treatment architecture, not the restart's definition of the job.

### Audience

The transcript's audience reading is more useful than the engine's demographic abstraction.

- Imagine a middle-school or high-school viewer, perhaps a high-school freshman as a prototype.
- Let the depicted characters be a little older than the core viewer: aspirational for younger viewers, present-tense for peers, and available to reminiscence for older viewers.
- Do not make the spot feel designed for six-year-olds.
- The references do not need to chase the newest meme. Familiar cultural shorthand can come from many periods.
- “Youthful” therefore means emotionally immediate, genre-aware, playful, and socially legible—not preschool-coded and not necessarily hyper-current.

This partly validates the existing age calibration, but it contradicts the strongest version of the old “present tense of being seventeen; no nostalgia plays” rule. The conversation explicitly allows looking ahead, inhabiting the moment, and looking back. It also actively reaches for *House*, Bosch, Ghibli, Dante, classic still life, and older animation.

### What “creative” appears to mean in this job

The most clarifying line in the transcript is that the brief says “creative,” but functionally means something closer to **magical and transformative**.

The characters do not have to demonstrate artistic production. They do not need to draw, build, or prove that they are creators. Eating the candy can grant entry into an authored burst of possibility: altered scale, a new body, a changed medium, a transformed environment, or an unexpected connection.

This resolves a concern the old research treated as a strategic gap: Peelerz doing creative things *to* passive characters is not necessarily a failure of the proposition. It may simply be how this treatment expresses the feeling of creativity. Character agency still matters dramatically—especially for Meg—but it does not need to be converted into a formal peel-choice system to make the brand statement valid.

### The emotional and narrative shape

The transcript supports a real but modest structural reading:

- Tina receives an individual physical release from a humdrum enclosure.
- Raul receives an individual heightened form or power fantasy, witnessed socially.
- Liz and John remain themselves while the shared world transforms; their connection is treated as the emotional pinnacle.
- All threads arrive above the clouds.

It also suggests an environmental escalation: partial or peripheral change can begin in Tina and Raul's worlds, then become total in the couple's sequence.

This does validate some of the previous “radius” and “social” analysis. But the transcript uses it as an observation about the current draft, not as a law that every alternative must preserve. The useful insight is **contrast and escalation**, not a rigid taxonomy.

### Hidden story versus visible plot

The conversation repeatedly asks for connective tissue without demanding that it be spelled out onscreen.

- Invent the logic between dream images.
- Give named characters enough imagined life that they feel real to the makers.
- Consider backstories, private worlds, and even absurd hidden premises.
- Ask whether Tina is somehow the author or dreamer of the chain.
- Let an invented story guide visual choices even if the audience never consciously receives it.

The old engine concentrated on visible mechanisms. The transcript adds a different creative substrate: **private narrative knowledge creates better images even when it is not exposition**.

### The desired reference behavior

References are not merely proof that a mechanism works. They are ingredients that make the visual world specific.

The conversation reaches for:

- Doctor Strange as an existing transition grammar;
- *Rick and Morty* as a self-aware recombination of familiar genre material;
- Studio Ghibli and *Castle in the Sky* for floating, overgrown, inhabited worlds;
- *House* for cheerful, psychedelic, handmade pop-surrealism;
- Bosch for dense, story-bearing image fields;
- classical still life and art-historical Easter eggs;
- anime and shōjo as differentiated character languages;
- claymation and 2D animation as tactile alternatives to generic pseudo-realistic 3D fruit characters;
- fairy tale, myth, Dante, and devouring/transformation stories as generators of offbeat imagery.

The transcript prefers **specific cultural texture over generic commercial polish**. A reference may be only faintly legible in the final result. Its job is to give the makers something concrete to think through, not necessarily to produce a recognizable homage.

### The working process implied by the conversation

The conversation proposes a much lighter and more human process than the engine:

1. Capture the wide brain-dump before editing it.
2. Work out a few rough visual mechanics.
3. Bring in mood boards and specific references to make those mechanics aesthetically interesting.
4. Imagine a longer version—the “ninety-second miniature world-saga”—even if the final commercial is thirty seconds.
5. Generate both the workable range and the “definitely not in the final edit, but great” range.
6. Let editing, not premature filtering, reduce the field.

This is not anti-structure. It is sequencing structure correctly.

## What the transcript validates in the existing work

### 1. Youthful without infantile

The previous work correctly recognized that the high-school and campus settings call for a youthful but not preschool register. Warnings about fuzzy generic mascots, mugging, and undifferentiated candyland remain useful as taste cautions, although they should not become automatic bans.

### 2. Tina's ordinary environment matters

The transcript strongly supports beginning with an uninteresting or gray outside world, then allowing transformation to approach Tina before the car opens. Existing attention to the car as enclosure and to visible outside-world change is well founded.

### 3. The couple should remain the stable emotional anchor

The Doctor Strange analysis correctly notices that bodies, trajectory, framing, color cues, or a camera orbit can remain invariant while worlds change. The transcript agrees that Liz and John themselves need not visibly transform; their shared environment carries the feeling.

### 4. Partial-to-total environmental change

The earlier radius analysis is too rigid as law, but it contains a useful staging insight: seed tropical or colorful transformation in the first two beats, then let the couple receive the fully transformed world.

### 5. Material facts matter when materials are depicted

The correction that both gummy layers are edible and that the material stretches rather than behaving like foil or paper remains essential. It prevents obvious product errors. It should guide depictions of the candy and candy-like material, not force every imaginative event to become gummy physics.

### 6. Renders are evidence about images, not evidence about ideas

The probe notes correctly separate the written concept from what the generated image actually communicates. The distinctions among a premise frame, a transformation action, and a world/mood frame are excellent production knowledge and should survive the restart.

### 7. Wide reference retrieval can be productive

The culture store's film, art, teen ritual, fairy-tale, and advertising fragments contain many strong things. The transcript explicitly supports drawing from both visual art and story. The problem is not the library; it is allowing the library's existing tags and peel-heavy selection to define the question.

## What the transcript destabilizes

### 1. The campaign-law search became the center too early

“Everything ordinary has another world under its surface” and “peel is progressive; whole is explosive” are plausible strategic concepts. The transcript does not establish either as the organizing truth of the job. The conversation barely treats peel-versus-whole as a branching personality system. It is more interested in the imaginative consequence, the world, the style, and the connective story.

Disposition: retain both as optional concept lanes, remove them from default authority.

### 2. Product causality was promoted from virtue to requirement

The old scorecards repeatedly ask whether another snack could sponsor the same image. That is a useful convergence question for some work. It is too strong as a generation requirement here.

The treatment itself moves among causal magic, affective magic, body fantasy, medium shifts, and brand-world spectacle. The transcript explicitly accepts that “creative” may mean magical and transformative rather than mechanically caused by peeling. A Ghibli-like island reference, a *House*-like transition, or a Boschian micro-scene may earn its place by shaping the feeling rather than proving product physics.

Disposition: make product causality one possible strength, not the admission ticket.

### 3. “One impossible law” was confused with “a few visual styleframes”

Ben needs images that can be summed up quickly. That does not mean the entire spot must obey a single physical law, or that every good idea must read fully in one frozen frame. The transcript thinks in sequences, transitions, repeated styles, hidden stories, and a longer imagined saga.

Disposition: keep clarity as a communication requirement; stop turning it into ontological austerity.

### 4. The membrane/molt reading is elegant but overdetermined

The membrane theory makes many elements rhyme: candy skin, car enclosure, body armor, shared worlds, medium shifts. The mid-molt audience reading is rhetorically attractive. Neither comes from Ben or the walk-and-talk. Once installed, that theory pulled the culture store and seed pool toward skins, layers, peels, clothes, shells, and thresholds.

Disposition: preserve as one authored essay or optional mood lens; do not use it to define what belongs.

### 5. The engine mistook balanced mechanism coverage for human coverage

The seed archive has:

- 41 Tina seeds;
- 24 Raul seeds;
- 20 Liz/John seeds;
- 3 finale seeds;
- no Meg category.

Meg appears in some seed text, but mainly as a witness, reaction, mouth, or contributor to Raul's event. The render prompts repeatedly prescribe her “dry” or “unimpressed” reaction. The transcript identifies this exact problem: Raul receives a full fantasy while Meg becomes a mouth-cavern into the next scene.

Disposition: the restart should treat **Raul and Meg as two imaginative subjects sharing one encounter**, not “Raul” as the slot and Meg as reaction design.

### 6. The process became highly articulate about process

The project accumulated phase walls, gates, postures, operators, ban states, quotas, archive niches, controlled vocabularies, metrics, and render adapters. Several are thoughtful. But the quantity of process language creates its own gravity: each new idea is asked to become a well-formed engine object before it has had time to be a funny image, a half-memory, a mood, or a line that makes David want to keep talking.

Disposition: freeze the engine as a research artifact. Do not implement or run it during the first restarted pass.

### 7. The machinery's anti-generic taste sometimes generated a generic house style

Many visual probes share the same premium-photoreal commercial look, product bag placement, clean central subjects, and explicit single-event staging. This makes comparison easier but also smooths away the transcript's appetite for stylistic rupture, art-historical residue, animation texture, and eccentric specificity.

Disposition: future image exploration should distinguish **mechanics tests** from **style/mood tests**. Not every frame should inherit the same commercial-VFX adapter.

## Beat-by-beat re-reading of the existing ideas

### Tina — much explored, but often through the wrong question

The old corpus asks, in effect, “What product-specific operation can transform Tina's car or street?” The transcript asks a more cinematic sequence of questions:

- What does she see before anything happens?
- How dull, gray, or ordinary is the Los Angeles environment?
- Does the world change while she fails to notice?
- Does transformation approach from outside and finally reach the car?
- What contrast makes her release feel exciting?
- What visual references make the transformed environment feel specific rather than generally colorful?

#### Existing Tina material with renewed value

- **Giant hand peeling the car** (`s037`, v01/e01): directly invited by Ben and discussed in the transcript. Keep as a baseline, not because it proves the membrane theory.
- **World changing outside before the car** (`s038`, `s065`): directly aligned with the transcript's outside-in suggestion.
- **Window/world sequence** (`s040`, v03 triptych): strongly aligned with thinking through multiple outside worlds and with the idea that a rough sequence can be represented as a multi-panel board.
- **Moving neighborhood stage-set** (batch 03): aligned with a boring filmed environment becoming visibly authored; its backstage comedy adds human texture.
- **Different hour at every house** (batch 03): a clear, gentle world-change ladder that does not require peel dogma.
- **Car skin becoming a canopy** (`s056`): compatible with the conversation's thought that the parachute might be peel-related, though the fleshy render proves the material treatment is unresolved.
- **Murals and tropical growth in a real LA street**: present in the transcript but barely developed in the seed/render corpus. This should be a fresh lane.

#### Tina material to demote

- The continuous ribbon (`s001`, T01, v02) is visually striking, but it accumulated protected-platform status because the engine valued a single causal material across scales. Treat it as one poetic image—“escape that remains attached”—rather than the solution to the scene or the whole edit.
- The stopped social-media feed (`s035`) and other interface metaphors feel generated from the engine's novelty search rather than from the conversation's visual appetite.
- Wrapper-paperweight imagery is factually wrong and conceptually peripheral.
- Sky inhale, shadow systems, gravity taxonomies, and ritual inspectors may remain in the nursery, but there is no reason to privilege them during the restart.

#### Tina gap

The existing Tina work has mechanics but little authored **place**. The transcript's gray-to-verdant LA, murals waking up, gem-like tropical vegetation, and a transformation Tina initially misses offer a more grounded visual-emotional spine than another abstract peel operation.

### Raul and Meg — the most important reset

The old corpus correctly notices that Ani-Mango-Raul can be visually generic if it is merely candy-colored armor. It explores multi-flavor mecha, social assembly, giant Raul, gummy Raul, capes, wings, and alternate avatar rules.

The transcript does not reject those ideas. It adds the missing half of the scene.

#### Existing Raul material with renewed value

- **Plain giant Raul eating little Raul** (`s044`, batch 02): directly present in Ben's note and the conversation. Its simple absurdity may be more useful than a repaired product-causal version.
- **Mango-gummy giant echo** (batch 03): a handsome variant, though converting the giant into candy makes the joke more branded and less strange. Both versions should coexist.
- **Multi-flavor Voltron** (`s043`): directly responsive to Ben and compatible with the conversation's desire not to leave other flavors behind.
- **Anime baseline** (`s046`) and style-specific transformations: the reference shortcut is permitted; the problem is only generic execution.
- **The stadium/world partly transforming** (`s075` and environmental fragments): aligned with the transcript's idea that beats one and two can plant seeds of the fully transformed world.

#### What must be reframed

“Social flavor mecha” gives the surrounding friends causal roles, but Meg still exists chiefly to supply a module and a dry face. The transcript asks a different question: what imaginative event is **hers**?

Fresh Meg lanes explicitly present in the conversation:

- a gummy fairy or character joyfully dives into her mouth and carries the camera;
- a tiny gummy Princess Meg or shōjo-world counterpart;
- a different flavor with its own visual language;
- a Boschian gummy demon whose descent turns the mouth cut into a 0.3-second hell-to-paradise passage;
- an active act of eating or receiving the transformed gummy rather than passive awe;
- a complementary transformation language that balances Raul's boy-coded power fantasy.

The mouth transition is not just a transition problem. It is a character-coverage problem. The restart should create paired Raul/Meg ideas before returning to Voltron optimization.

### Liz and John — closest to the transcript's true visual playground

The old corpus repeatedly tried to make the couple's world changes product-causal through peel films, redraw seams, and shared skins. Those are valid possibilities, but the transcript is more interested in what the worlds feel like, how one shades into another, and how specific reference cultures can inhabit the sequence.

#### Existing couple material with renewed value

- **Campus rotates around them** (batch 03): strong “familiar but strange” logic. The pair stay emotionally still while the environment performs the event.
- **The stable-couple/two-world baseline** (batch 02): useful formal demonstration even though the image reads like a mural or split screen.
- **Triptych and invariant tests**: proof that identity can persist across world changes.
- **Stop-motion pantry campus** (batch 03): useful as a tactile medium fragment, though the execution skews cozy/juvenile and needs a more specific visual reference.
- **Script-native mango grove, animation, underwater, and claymation** (`s048–s051`): retain as starting nouns, not finished worlds.
- **Wearing each other's worlds** (`s080`): a genuine relationship idea rather than merely a peel mechanism; worth rediscovering without its system language.

#### Couple material to demote

- The golden peel portal/redraw images repeatedly become generic brand swooshes. The current renders do not justify further still-image repair.
- The insistence that each world be literally peeled from the previous one is not grounded strongly enough to constrain the whole sequence.
- Candy city, fruit space, and other world-menu entries are inventories, not yet perspectives.

#### Couple gap

The transcript supplies an aesthetic program the existing visuals have barely tested:

- *House*-like cheerful psychedelic compositing;
- a less harsh alternative to superhero-movie universe switching;
- transitions that brighten, dim, and flux rather than only smash-cut;
- Ghibli-like verdant ruins and floating-world atmosphere;
- Bosch or still-life fragments as Easter eggs inside a larger scene;
- a visual rhyme from glowing mangoes in trees to glowing gummy fish in kelp;
- a specific balance of tropical green, gem-like fruit color, underwater blue, and candy sparkle;
- the couple as the constant while styles—not just locations—change.

That is where a mood/reference pass may contribute more than additional mechanism generation.

### Finale — underdeveloped because the engine preferred solvable local mechanisms

Only three of the 88 archived seeds use `finale` as their slot. The previous theory talked extensively about a functioning Peelerz ecology, but comparatively little finale ideation or rendering was done.

The transcript's finale material is modest but useful:

- floating islands and airship-world references;
- *Castle in the Sky* and overgrown Ghibli-scale ruins;
- the wider Gummyverse rather than a world containing only branded gummies;
- the accumulated characters flying or arriving together;
- the possibility of small gummy societies, rituals, games, and creatures as optional delight material.

The Bosch-garden render (v05) uses some of these nouns but lands as generic pastel storybook candyland. It has fruit houses and many activities, but little Boschian density, tension, compositional strangeness, or art-historical specificity. It is evidence that naming a reference in a prompt does not produce its imaginative function.

The restart should not begin by deciding that the finale must be a “functioning peel ecology.” First decide what kind of place it is emotionally and visually: destination, revelation, playground, ancient gummy civilization, floating archipelago, shared dream, or one-second super-wide joke.

### Useful but needing a different question

- **Multi-flavor mecha:** legible and on brief; ask what is specific about Raul and Meg, not only how modules assemble.
- **Mango giant echo:** attractive but less funny than the plain giant; perhaps a different beat in the escalation rather than a replacement.
- **Stop-motion pantry campus:** a medium test; needs a precise artistic world and older performance register.

### Evidence of canalization or mismatch

- **Gummy canopy:** anatomical/fleshy rather than joyful or confectionary.
- **Peel-film/redraw portals:** the model repeatedly renders a generic golden frame around an alternate background.
- **Stopped-feed interface:** understandable as a tech metaphor, weak as the transcript's kind of magic.
- **Paperweight wrapper:** both wrong about the object and disconnected from the strongest human material.
- **Social flavor mecha:** conceptually tidy, but turns every person into a functional input and leaves character interiority untouched.
- **Bosch garden:** reference name without reference function; visually too generic and young.

### A general visual problem

The repeated package-forward photoreal register makes many frames look like finished candy advertisements before the imagined world has acquired a point of view. Product visibility matters, but the same package placement, clean commercial lighting, centered teen, and isolated VFX gag create a visual sameness that the seed diversity metrics do not detect.

Future exploration should allow:

- reference frames with no product packaging at all;
- diagrams and collages that test transition logic;
- painterly or animated moodframes;
- dense environment studies;
- character-expression studies;
- frame pairs and mini-sequences;
- deliberately non-final images made to discover a style.

## Important material the old corpus missed or underused

### Meg as an imaginative subject

This is the clearest omission and should be addressed first.

### Characters as people before mechanisms

The transcript proposes names, backstories, desires, private references, and hidden relationships as scaffolding. The archive mostly stores actions and rules.

### Specific visual taste

The conversation contains strong preferences—against pseudo-realistic generic 3D fruit, toward cutesy 2D possibilities, handmade or historical textures, specific art references, and images that reward looking. The old system mostly treated tone as a safety and age register.

### The distinction between usable and enjoyable

Ben needs things he can work with, but the conversation also wants to give him things he will enjoy seeing. This legitimizes a wild appendix rather than forcing every image to pass the same convergence gate.

### Reference-to-world translation

The old store contains reference fragments, but its workflow commonly extracts a mechanism from each. The transcript also wants palette, texture, compositional density, emotional temperature, transition rhythm, and cultural residue.

### Visual rhymes across worlds

Glowing mango fruit becoming glowing gummy fish, trees becoming kelp, or one composition surviving a material change is a gentler and more aesthetic connective strategy than a single literal peel strip crossing every sequence.

### The longer invisible version

Designing a ninety-second inner saga can make a thirty-second spot feel as though it belongs to a larger world. The old engine generated isolated seeds and short ladders but not a full imaginative cut built for discovery.

## A lightweight restart

The next pass does not need a new engine. It needs four human encounter sheets and a disciplined separation between discovery and selection.

### Step 1 — make four encounter sheets

One each for:

1. Tina in the car;
2. Raul and Meg in the bleachers;
3. Liz and John on campus;
4. the Peelerz Islands convergence.

Each sheet should contain only:

- what is source-fixed;
- what the current draft does;
- what the walk-and-talk notices or questions;
- emotional possibilities;
- character possibilities;
- visual/reference possibilities;
- unresolved questions.

No score, campaign law, operator, or product-causality requirement.

### Step 2 — make three lanes for each encounter

#### Script-close

Clarify, enrich, or restyle what is already there: giant hand, Ani-Mango-Raul, mouth dive, world shifts, floating islands.

#### Lateral

Change the mechanism or hidden story while preserving the encounter and emotional role.

#### Gift/wild

Make the images that are probably not in the final edit but reveal taste, joy, or a surprising world: Boschian gummy demon, fruit philosophers, mango-gummy pyramid society, *House* transition, 16-bit Gummyverse, fairy-tale talisman.

This structure honors both the usable and enjoyable ranges without pretending they should be judged identically.

### Step 3 — work in four passes, not one generator loop

1. **People and hidden story:** Who are they? What private fantasy or dynamic is being amplified?
2. **Images and references:** What paintings, films, animation styles, materials, palettes, and compositions make that fantasy specific?
3. **Transformation:** What actually changes, and how might it unfold?
4. **Transitions and convergence:** What visual rhyme or motion hands us to the next encounter?

The old work largely started at pass 3.

### Step 4 — build the long invisible cut

Write or thumbnail a deliberately overlong version in which each encounter has enough time to develop. This is not a proposed runtime. It is a way to discover what the thirty-second compression implies rather than starting with compression.

### Step 5 — only then revisit the old archive

After a fresh human-led pass, compare it against the 88 seeds and existing renders. Salvage forgotten ideas without allowing old labels or votes to dictate selection. The archive becomes a memory aid and mutation source, not the room's agenda.

### Step 6 — convergence questions

When it is finally time to select, ask:

- Does the image or sequence make us want to see more?
- Does it feel youthful without feeling designed for little children?
- Is there a clear emotional or comic pleasure?
- Is the world visually specific rather than generically “creative”?
- Can Ben use it, enjoy it, or use something inside it?
- Can it be communicated in a few boards, even if the action needs more than one frame?
- Is the candy present and appetizing where it needs to be?
- Does the idea help this encounter differ from the others while still belonging in the same spot?

Possible secondary questions, not universal gates:

- Is the Peelerz action causally responsible?
- Does peel versus whole matter?
- Is there one dominant physical law?
- Could another snack sponsor it?
- Does it strengthen a campaign-wide visual rhyme?

## What should happen to the creativity engine

Do not delete it. Do not keep refining it during this job.

The engine can later be tested on a narrower need, for example:

- “We have six good Tina ideas but they all transform the street in the same way; force lateral alternatives.”
- “We need three strange mouth-transition mechanisms after Meg's character is established.”
- “Our couple worlds all feel like location swaps; retrieve medium and composition references.”

That is a healthier role: a tool called because the creative room has diagnosed a problem. It should not be the room itself.
